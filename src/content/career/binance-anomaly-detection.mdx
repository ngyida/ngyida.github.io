## context

The team was working on machine-learning-driven anomaly detection in a large operational environment. That meant technical decisions were closely tied to incident response, alert quality, and the speed at which people could make sense of noisy systems.

## what I built

I contributed to several parts of the backend platform:

- a root-cause analysis flow that used monitoring data to help incident managers narrow down impacted areas
- a real-time knowledge base pipeline to support time-sensitive internal and Web3 context for LLM-assisted workflows
- an alert aggregation mechanism to reduce redundant or low-signal notifications
- a better feedback loop so incident managers could help improve the anomaly engine over time

## what mattered technically

The most interesting challenge was not simply collecting more data. It was deciding what form of backend support actually helped operators. A system can surface huge volumes of information and still fail if it does not reduce cognitive load at the moment someone needs to act.

Working on the knowledge base also made retrieval quality feel very concrete. Timeliness, structure, and domain specificity matter a lot more when the target user is responding to an incident instead of casually exploring information.

## what I learned

This role strengthened my appreciation for backend systems that sit close to operations. I enjoyed building features where the impact could be measured in response quality and time saved, not just throughput or architecture elegance.
